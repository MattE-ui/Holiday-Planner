# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

You and your family/partner, planning a trip together. The context is relaxed and
domestic — at home, not under time pressure — weighing a handful of holidays you've
already researched before committing real money to one. The job to be done is **deciding
together**: comparing locations and options, understanding what each trip really costs
(total and per person), and arriving at a choice everyone feels good about.

## Product Purpose

Holiday Planner turns scattered holiday research into one calm, browsable place to
**compare trips before booking**. You browse Trips → Locations → Holidays; each holiday
lays out accommodation, flights, car hire, and a combined price breakdown. Costs left
unconfirmed show honestly as "to confirm" so partly-researched options still display
cleanly. Success looks like: the place sells itself through imagery, the numbers are
trustworthy at a glance, and the family lands on a confident decision without the chaos
of twelve browser tabs.

## Brand Personality

Warm, editorial, inviting — three words: **warm, editorial, considered**. The voice is a
well-travelled friend with good taste, not a booking engine: it shows the holiday rather
than selling it, and tells the truth about cost. Calm and aspirational, never urgent.
The reference feel is Ikos Resorts and travel-magazine editorial: full-bleed photography,
generous whitespace, restrained type, a quiet seafoam/teal accent — luxury through
restraint, not ornament.

## Anti-references

- **Loud OTA / booking sites** (Expedia, Booking.com): urgency banners, "1 left!",
  scarcity nudges, ad density, clutter. None of it. There is no one to rush here.
- **Generic AI-SaaS template**: cream/sand body backgrounds, a tracked-uppercase eyebrow
  above every section, identical icon-heading-text card grids, gradient-text accents,
  the hero-metric template.
- **Corporate / sterile dashboard**: cold gray enterprise admin chrome, dense tables with
  no warmth, design that feels like data entry instead of trip-planning.

## Design Principles

1. **Imagery does the selling.** Let the place sell itself with full-bleed, high-quality
   photography; chrome and UI recede around it. This is the heart of the project.
2. **Aspirational, but honest about cost.** Evoke the holiday *and* keep the price
   breakdown clear and trustworthy — the decision is real money, and clarity builds trust.
3. **Calm over urgency.** Space, pacing, and restraint create relaxed anticipation. No
   scarcity, no countdowns, no shouting.
4. **Editorial restraint.** Generous whitespace, considered typography, one confident
   voice. Nothing decorative for its own sake.
5. **Comparison without coldness.** Make weighing options feel like flipping through a
   travel magazine, not filling a spreadsheet — even when the underlying job is comparison.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target **WCAG 2.1 AA**: body text ≥4.5:1 contrast, large text ≥3:1, full keyboard access,
visible focus states. Imagery-heavy layouts must keep text legible over photographs (use
scrims/overlays, never rely on the photo being dark enough). Price status (confirmed vs.
"to confirm") must not rely on color alone — pair it with text or an icon. Provide
meaningful alt text for location and accommodation photography.
